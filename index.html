About

I am an economist at the University of California, Irvine. My research and teaching interests are in macroeconomics, monetary policy, and banking.

At UC, Irvine, I teach courses in the principles of microeconomics and macroeconomics, intermediate macroeconomics, money and banking, financial markets, and asymmetric information.

Currently, I am serving as Director of a newly-established economics tutoring center called the Economics Learning Center.

Before coming to California, I spent two years in Missoula, Montana where I was Visiting Assistant Professor of Economics at the University of Montana. I received my PhD in Economics from the University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill where I wrote my dissertation under Michael K. Salemi.

fredclass Python module

fredclass is a Python module that I have written for retrieving and working with data from Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED). The module makes it easy to download specific data series and provides a set of tools for transforming the data in order to construct plots and perform statistical analysis. The fredclass module is useful for anyone doing empirical research using the data available from FRED and for anyone, e.g. economics teachers and journalists, that will benefit from having an efficient and flexible way to access FRED with Python.

The primary advantage of the fredclass module is that the user can easily access a FRED series using the unique series ID. For example, a plot of the US unemployment rate is easily created with the following code:


              from fredclass import fred, quickplot   
              unemp = fred('UNRATE') 
              quickplot(unemp,recess=True,save=True,name='fredclass_example1')

Teaching with Animations

In general we teach students about economic dynamics by presenting models that are inherently static and then asking our students to imagine how the models explain a world that moves through time.

For example, we might explain business cycle dynamics to students with the IS-LM model. But the model is drawn on a chalkboard or on paper and so each student has to visualize their own version of the dynamic story.

When possible, I prefer to show my students how the models I teach them are dynamic by preparing animations computed from the equilibrium conditions of the model. For example, in the video above, I show how the Solow model implies movement of the equilibrium through time. With the animation, students can see that the model really does converge to a steady state. Moreover, students can see the slowing growth rate as the economy approaches the steady state.

I have also found that animations are useful for presenting time series data. We want students to think of economic quantities that are moving through time, but we show them a line graph or a scatter plot with stationary data points. We ask them to imagine how the graphs might change when new data are added.

Like the exclusive use of chalkboard models, static data plots can obscur the point: we exist as part of a dynamic system.

Having taught the yield curve a number of times, I found that I was asking students to take my word about statements like "yield curves are typically upward-sloping" or "the yields on bonds with different times to maturity move together."

The video above is an animation of the daily yield curves for the US starting from 1965. Rather than telling students facts that they cannot readily verify, I now show them all off the historical yield curves. The animation provides a basis for class discussion: how many of the yield curve properties can the students uncover on their own from the animation?

Please checkout at my YouTube channel to see more examples of my economic model animations. The Python code for the yield curve animation is available from the Github repository for my fredclass module for Python.

Solow Model Simulation Tool

The Solow Model Simulation Tool is a web-based tool designed to help students learn how to use the Solow growth model. The tool allows students to create their own custom transition paths under different parameter configurations. The value any variable for a specific time period is revealed by hovering the pointer over the line.

The Solow Model Simulation Tool is an excellent resource for students that are learning about economic growth models. The tool is also a natural starting poitn for a homework assignment on economic growth.

Economics Learning Center

The Economics Learning Center (ELC) is a new initiative of the Department of Economics at UC, Irvine. The ELC is a walk-in tutoring facility available to any UCI student currently enrolled in the microeconomics and macroeconomics principles courses and also intermediate microeconomics.

The ELC is open from Monday to Friday from 1 to 9pm. Most of the time, the ELC is staffed by two graduate Teaching Assistants and two undergraduate Peer Tutors. The purpose of the ELC is to provide our undergraduates with a place in which they can expect to meet up with their peers to work on homework assignments, to review lecture notes, and to studey for upcoming exams.

As Director of the ELC, I have been responsible for developing explicit objectives of the tutoring center, preparing staff manuals for the graduate Teaching Assistants and undergraduate Peer Tutors, recruiting and training the Peer Tutors, and overseeing the operation of the center.

For more information about the ELC, take a look at the website or come visit us in 165 Social Science Tower (SST) during the week!
